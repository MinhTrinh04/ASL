# ABSTRACT

Sign language is an essential communication tool for the deaf and hard-of-hearing community. However, learning this language through traditional methods such as photo books or videos is often challenging due to the three-dimensional visual nature and movement of hand gestures. Learners lack a realistic multi-dimensional interactive environment and a real-time feedback mechanism to self-correct their errors. Regarding current sign language education applications in virtual reality, most solutions still depend on physical controllers, which obstruct natural hand movements. The combination of these issues raises the demand for a virtual reality learning solution that optimizes hand interactions, making the self-learning process as effective and natural as possible.

This graduation thesis proposes the development of an interactive Sign Language Lecture system in Virtual Reality. By fully leveraging hand tracking technology, the application allows learners to use their physical hands to simulate and form sign language gestures. This approach opens up a vivid visual learning method through the integration of Edgar Dale's Cone of Experience and David Kolb's Experiential Learning Theory, helping to transform gesture images into long-term muscle memory and supporting learners in acquiring the language naturally and sustainably.

The project's solution includes building a vivid three-dimensional virtual reality learning space that offers an intuitive interactive environment for learners. The system integrates template-guided hand posture practice modes and various competency assessment tests, contributing to knowledge consolidation and enhancing self-learning efficiency.

The main contribution of the thesis is a pioneering controller-free interactive virtual reality lecture that applies hand tracking technology to the field of special education, specifically supporting American Sign Language. The product not only harmoniously combines virtual reality technology with pedagogical principles to deliver an empathetic, intuitive, and accessible learning experience for the general public, but also opens up a new development path for natural interaction VR applications serving the community. This solution contributes to bridging the communication gap and fostering sustainable inclusion for the deaf and hard-of-hearing.

**Keywords** _Virtual Reality (VR), American Sign Language (ASL), Gesture Recognition, Unity XR Hands, Educational Technology (EdTech), Controller-free Interaction._
